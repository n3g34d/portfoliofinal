Iterative Design

This project involves iteratively designing a webpage for tech startup, Slai

Overview

While enrolled in CSCI1300 at Brown, I've learned that the process of web design is comprehensive. The websites we view online are the results of many drafts, various sources of feedback, and numerous iterations. As a group, three of my classmates and I worked together to attempt this iterative web-design process for tech startup, Slai. Together we researched Slai's mission, created mockups, gathered peer feedback, and conducted user testing with random individuals to ultimately construct a product that we could present to Slai founders. This project provided us valuable experience with website creation and user friendly design.

Process

Below I've summarized my five step project process. Click through the slides to learn more!

Step 1: Selecting a Startup

For this assignment, students were tasked with selecting an interesting startup with a clear concept and description. My group and I chose a startup called Slai.

Slai is a prototyping platform for machine learning development. They provide a self-serve service for developers to build and ship models from their browser, and publish templates that anyone can use.

As students studying computer science, we selected Slai for their purpose that aligned with our shared interest. We also saw opporunity for creativity as we weren't aware of many similar concepts.

Step 2: Creating Sketches

Our group began by sketching possible designs for the startup. We were instructed not to look at the current website to ensure our designs are creative and not simply redesigns/improvements.

The three images to the right are sketches designed by our group. They all are slightly different, yet unsurprisingly they all contain a home page and text editor page.

Step 3: Combining Sketches Into a Low Fidelity Mockup

Once we had each created our sketches, we collectively created a mockup using a web design tool called Balsamiq. This application allowed us to create "rough drafts" where we could experiment with basic design. Balsamiq thus allows for "Low fi mockups" as they are simple and lack much color or design.

Step 4: High Fidelity Mockups and Critique

After initial designs, our group created a mockup with Figma, an online collaborative web design tool. Our initial version was similar to the low fi mockup.

We then presented this first Figma version to a group of UI/UX students. The feedback that we received was crucial in the final iteration of our prototype. Some notable feedback included:

  • The interactions users had with buttons weakened learnability (Some buttons initially led to unrelated pages)
  • Returning to previous pages was difficult (There initially was no back button)
  • The "Home" and "Templates" page seemed to be switched (In the initial version, templates existed on both pages)
  • There was no way to actually contact the company (The contact feature was not built out)
  • The intuitiveness of the navigation sidebar could be improved (The sidebar initially did not expand and was un-labeled)

    • We addressed all of these changes by adding buttons, deleting pages, combining Home/Templates, and more. Check out our final version to the right!

    Step 5: User Testing

    After revising our third iteration based on user feedback, we submitted the fourth iteration to a site where random users review our website by completing some specified tasks.

    Users were given the following tasks:

  • Share the “Neural Networks” directory from the Files page
  • Begin editing with a Decision Tree template
  • Read about the website and company from the “About Us” page

    • Here is summarized feedback from three of the random testers:

    • User 1
      • Very easy to share files. Some user interactions are confusing and not entirely intuitive. Some wording on the prototype is ambiguous.
    • User 2
      • Navigation of site was easy.Template selection should be clearer on Home page. Incredibly easy to find information on company.
    • User 3
      • Share button should also be on individual files. User became confused by Figma layout and was not able to complete last two tasks

    Overall the feedback was mostly expected. We were surprised however, that the second user gave advice that contradicted our peers. The second user indicated a preference for a more clear Home vs Template page while our classmates suggested we combine the pages. It's fascinating to see that usability is not as clear cut as it appears.

    Conclusion

    This project gave me a thorough introduction to the iterative web design process. Our group learned a lot about optimal web design from each, peers, and user testers. Following the completion of user testing, a member of our group emailed the startup with our findings. We were very pleased to recieve a response from the founders and happy to hear they enjoyed our seamless integration of the text editor sandbox.

    Takeaways:

    • Multiple iterations are key to designing a seamless project
    • Feedback and critique regarding web design can be contradicting
    • Participating in user testing is a great way to observe users iteract with your design
